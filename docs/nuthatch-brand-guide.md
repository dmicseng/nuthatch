# Nuthatch Brand Voice & Identity Guide

*Internal reference for the team. Use when writing copy, naming features, or designing surfaces.*

---

## 1. Brand personality

Four words that define how Nuthatch shows up:

**Quietly confident** — We don't shout. We know our work is good and let it speak. No hype, no breathless promises, no "revolutionize your workflow."

**Protective** — Your data is your data. We care about it like the bird cares about its stash — memorizing every location, watching for threats, moving things when observed.

**Smart** — Nuthatches are small but have one of the largest brain-to-body ratios of any bird. They remember hundreds of cache sites. The product reflects this: thoughtful defaults, clever integrations, no feature bloat.

**Crafted** — Built with care, not scale-at-all-costs. We'd rather have 100 customers who love it than 10,000 who tolerate it.

---

## 2. Voice pillars

### Clear over clever
First draft is usually too clever. Second draft says the same thing in half the words. Third draft is right.

*Weak*: "Unleash unprecedented visibility into your distributed cost landscape."
*Strong*: "See every subscription and API bill in one place."

### Specific over vague
Numbers, names, and nouns beat adjectives. "Fast" means nothing; "under 50ms" means something.

*Weak*: "Works with all your favorite tools."
*Strong*: "AWS, GCP, Azure, OpenAI, Anthropic, Stripe, and 14 more."

### Plainspoken over corporate
Talk like a smart friend who works in the industry, not a marketing department.

*Weak*: "Leveraging our proprietary algorithm to optimize spend efficiency."
*Strong*: "Flags subscriptions that haven't been used in 90 days."

### Quietly confident over promotional
State facts. Let the reader decide they're impressive.

*Weak*: "The #1 self-hosted cost tracker, trusted by thousands!"
*Strong*: "Self-hosted. Open source. Built for teams."

## 3. Voice do's and don'ts

**Do**:
- Use bird metaphors sparingly and when they actually help (cache, stash, territory, flock)
- Write in sentence case — never Title Case, never ALL CAPS
- Start sentences with verbs when giving instructions
- Use contractions (you're, we're, it's) — they make copy sound human
- Keep paragraphs to 3 sentences max in product copy

**Don't**:
- Don't over-milk the bird theme — a "tweet" button would be too cute, a "nest" would too
- Don't use "seamlessly", "frictionless", "supercharge", "unleash", "empower", "revolutionize", "game-changer"
- Don't use emoji in product UI (OK in marketing in moderation; never in app)
- Don't exclaim!! Calm confidence doesn't need punctuation props
- Don't refer to users as "users" in user-facing copy. "You" or "your team" instead

## 4. Tagline candidates

12 options across different angles. Pick the one that feels most like you when you read it out loud.

### Bird metaphor angle
1. **Stash your stack costs.** — short, memorable, uses "stash" which is nuthatch-native
2. **Cache your costs. See what others can't.** — developer pun (cache), metaphor clear
3. **Remember every bill. Hide every key.** — two-sentence, rhythmic, protection-forward
4. **A small bird with a sharp memory for your stack.** — narrative, charming, longer

### Self-host / privacy angle
5. **Your stack costs. Your server. Your rules.** — three-part, ownership-forward
6. **Spend tracking that stays on your side of the glass.** — metaphor-light, strong visual
7. **Know your costs. Keep them yours.** — parallel structure, ownership

### Outcome angle
8. **One dashboard for every bill.** — plain, direct, functional
9. **Stop paying for things you forgot you bought.** — pain-forward, relatable
10. **See every subscription, cloud bill, and API charge. In one place.** — specific, demonstrative

### Quietly confident angle
11. **Built for teams that read their own bills.** — positions audience (smart, hands-on)
12. **Cost tracking, quietly.** — minimalist, voice-consistent

**My top 3 picks**:
- **#2 "Cache your costs. See what others can't."** — best blend of dev wink, story, privacy
- **#5 "Your stack costs. Your server. Your rules."** — strongest positioning statement
- **#11 "Built for teams that read their own bills."** — audience-defining, memorable

**For the landing page hero**, use your current line or #2. For social bio / one-liner in decks, use #5 or #11.

## 5. Microcopy patterns

Small wording choices that add up to brand feel.

### Buttons
- Primary action: verb + object ("Add service", "Import receipts", "Join early access")
- Avoid: "Submit", "Click here", "Go"
- Avoid: "Get started" as primary CTA — too generic

### Empty states
Two sentences max. First sentence explains what's missing. Second sentence offers the next step.

- *Weak*: "No subscriptions yet. Click the button to add one!"
- *Strong*: "Nothing tracked yet. Add your first service or paste a receipt."

### Error states
Plain language. Own the problem. Say what to do.

- *Weak*: "Error: Invalid input (code 422)"
- *Strong*: "That email address doesn't look right. Check for typos and try again."

### Success states
Acknowledge. Don't celebrate.

- *Weak*: "🎉 Awesome! Your subscription has been successfully added!"
- *Strong*: "Added. Next charge: Dec 14."

### Loading states
Specific, not generic.

- *Weak*: "Loading..."
- *Strong*: "Syncing with Stripe..."

### Feature naming
Use real words, not invented terms. A feature that "detects unused subscriptions" should be called "Unused services", not "ZombieGuard™".

## 6. Terminology cheat sheet

Small decisions on what to call things. Consistency matters more than cleverness.

| Concept | Use | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| A service you pay for | Service | Tool, app, vendor (unless specifically the company) |
| The cost over a period | Spend | Expense, expenditure, outlay |
| A user in the app | Member | User (in UI), seat (in UI) |
| A company / workspace | Organization, org | Tenant, account, workspace (too generic) |
| Finding something unusual | Flag | Alert, notify (both fine in alerts context) |
| A dashboard section | View | Panel, widget, module |
| The act of saving a record | Add | Create (more technical, OK in API) |

## 7. Visual identity (companion to voice)

### Color palette
- **Background** `#f5f0e6` — warm cream, like pale bark
- **Surface** `#fbf8f1` — lighter cream for cards
- **Text primary** `#1c1a15` — near-black, warm
- **Accent deep** `#2b3d4f` — slate blue, the nuthatch's crown stripe
- **Accent warm** `#a8743c` — ochre, the nuthatch's belly
- **Border** `#d9d0bf` — warm stone

Rule: ochre is for highlights, CTAs, and featured elements. Slate is for structural elements and accents. Most of the page is cream + near-black.

### Typography
- **Display** — Instrument Serif (italic for emphasis) — feels like old field-guide plates
- **Body** — Geist (regular + medium) — clean, modern sans
- **Mono** — JetBrains Mono — for numbers, code, metadata

Never more than 2 weights per font. No 700, no 800. Italic serif is the only decorative move.

### Logo mark
Simple nuthatch silhouette in profile, head-down (the bird's signature pose — only bird that climbs down trees head-first). Works as small favicon. Can be abstracted to just the crown stripe as a secondary mark.

## 8. Where voice shows up

Checklist for keeping voice consistent across surfaces:

- [ ] Landing page headlines and hero copy
- [ ] Button labels throughout the app
- [ ] Error messages and validation states
- [ ] Email notifications (transactional + digest)
- [ ] GitHub README and issue templates
- [ ] Documentation (tutorials, API reference)
- [ ] Social bio (Twitter/X, LinkedIn, GitHub org)
- [ ] Sales and pricing page copy
- [ ] Onboarding flow
- [ ] Empty states

If a surface feels off, it's usually because it slipped into one of the *don't* patterns. Read out loud. If you wouldn't say it to a colleague at lunch, rewrite.
